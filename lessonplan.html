Lesson Plan
Upward Round Kicks

Updated: 12/7/2025

Objectives

This week’s lesson is focused on Upward Round Kicks. Students will learn how to strike with their shins for a quick and powerful attack.

Upward Round Kick: An upward Round Kick is a variation of the Round Kick that strikes at an angle rather than sideways. This form of kick is commonly used in Muay Thai* and is halfway between a Snap Front Kick and a Full-Turn Round Kick. It strikes at a 45-degree angle upward. A Full-Turn Round Kick strikes horizontally at 90 degrees, an Upward Round Kick strikes diagonally at 45 degrees, and a Snap Front Kick strikes vertically at 0 degrees. The supporting foot pivots slightly to compensate for the body’s movement. Students will primarily practice this technique in a Front or Mid Stance using their back leg.

*Muay Thai is a martial arts style from Thailand.

Mid Stance: A Mid Stance is halfway between a Front Stance and a Back (Guarding) Stance in terms of the feet and legs. It is neutral and can be used for offense, defense, and maneuvering. The weight is evenly distributed between both feet. The lead foot faces the front while the back foot angles slightly outward, allowing easy transitions depending on the movement.

Drills

Line Drill: Students line up single file in a Mid Stance and advance toward the instructor holding a striking shield. The first student will Upward Round Kick the pad several times. The instructor may add a blocker pad for variety. After a few strong techniques, the student will run to the back of the line while the others advance. This drill is done on both sides, and students must maintain their stance, guard, and a slight bounce to stay in motion.

Close-Range Exchange: Because this kick is shorter-range, it is ideal for mitt work. The instructor will lightly swipe at the students with focus mitts, requiring blocks (wing block or upper middle block) or evasions. Mitts may also be held for punches. When the mitts are held to the side at an angle, the student immediately performs an Upward Round Kick into both mitts. Students work to build rhythm, speed, and reaction time.

Games

Best Kicker: The instructor holds a pad for each student to perform an Upward Round Kick. The class may stand in a line or semi-circle. Each student does one kick and freezes in their stance. After everyone has kicked, the instructor announces the winner. To win, the student must perform the best technique and hold their stance. After several rounds of Upward Round Kicks, the game can expand to other moves.

Nobody Wins: Students line up side by side in a guarding stance. When the instructor says “Kick!”, each student performs a back-leg Upward Round Kick and lands in a new guarding stance. They travel down the floor this way, alternating lead legs (north/south paw). Any error—dropping the guard, weak kick, balance loss, forgetting “Aiya!”, incorrect fist—forces a complete restart. The game is impossible to win, teaching unattainable perfection and dramatically improving their kicks.

Words of Wisdom

Mastering the Upward Round Kick reminds us of the importance of adaptability in close quarters. In life we often face situations that require us to adjust and find strength in proximity—difficult conversations, challenging work projects, or personal relationships. Effectiveness up close is a crucial life skill.

At-Home Challenge of the Week

Cushion Control Kick: Parents kneel and hold a cushion securely at a safe height. Students practice striking the cushion with their shins using an Upward Round Kick. The goal is precision and controlled power. This helps refine technique and improves the student’s ability to regulate force.

Parent-Child Bonding Idea

Teach A(nother) Technique: Have your child teach you everything they know about the Upward Round Kick. Ask thoughtful questions that require them to reflect and explain. Teaching is one of the strongest ways to learn. If your child is too young to explain verbally, learn together by copying movements.
