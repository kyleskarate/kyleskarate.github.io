Lesson Plan
Side Kicks

Updated: 12/7/2025

Objectives

This week’s lesson is focused on Side Kicks. This kick is done in a Side Stance and shoots forward as a direct strike.

Side Stance: A Side Kick is best done from a Side Stance, so this will be practiced first. Students will be taught how to transition into this stance easily and why it is advantageous for this technique.

Facing sideways, the lead foot points back toward the student at a 45-degree angle, while the back toes face the rear. The lead leg is mostly straight, while the back leg is bent. Having the back toes face the rear allows the back knee to bend more when leaning back, preventing loss of balance. While in this position, the guard naturally shifts toward the back with the hips. To complete the stance, the torso twists in the opposite direction so the guard faces forward again.

Side Kicks: Students will learn proper Side Kick technique. They will practice kicking directly from a Side Stance and maneuvering from other stances to achieve the kick. It is not always possible to enter the perfect stance, so understanding the motions is essential. There is no back-leg version of this kick due to its slowness. Students learn about direct force and how the unique direction of a Side Kick makes back-leg versions too slow, while lead-leg Side Kicks are fast and effective.

Students accustomed to kicking with the back leg may feel awkward at first using their lead leg—this is good practice for weight shifting, leaning, and lead-leg kicking. The class will revisit foot position, speed, and power.

The Side Kick involves lifting the lead leg with the knee toward the chest and toes pulled up (dorsiflexion). The heel arches and launches toward the target while the body stays sideways and the leg remains nearly parallel to the floor. Lifting too high jeopardizes balance. The foot position is similar to but distinct from a Push Front Kick. The motion will be demonstrated clearly so students can mimic the form.

Students also practice balancing on one foot to improve control. Side Kicks will be practiced on both striking shields and the clapper pad.

Drills

Kicking By Numbers: Most kicks can be broken into four steps. Students stand in a Side Stance by a wall, place one hand on it, and kick using the opposite leg. The instructor calls each step:

  • One: Lift up your leg
  • Two: Extend it out
  • Three: Bring it back
  • Four: Put it down

Students repeat on both sides. Some enjoy leading and calling out numbers.

Three-Kick Drill: Students kick the shield three times in a row, focusing on speed and power. This drill develops the reflex to return the foot to its starting point, preventing balance loss. Because the shield does not clap, correct technique and power are required to make sound—encouraging proper foot position and strong kicks.

Slide-In Side Kick: If students progress well, they learn a slide-in variation. This adds motion, covers distance, conceals the kick, and increases power. It prepares students for more advanced transitions.

Games

Nobody Wins: Students line up in a guarding stance. When the instructor says “Kick!”, they perform a slide-in Side Kick and land in a new Side Stance, traveling forward. Any mistake—incorrect fist, dropped guard, balance loss, incorrect knee motion, forgetting “Aiya!”—requires restarting. The instructor becomes stricter the farther they go. The game is impossible to win, teaching the ideal of unattainable perfection and dramatically improving technique.

Words of Wisdom

Mastering the Side Kick demonstrates that complex skills are built on foundational ones. Just as this kick requires first mastering a solid Side Stance, life often requires fundamental knowledge or personal grounding before bigger achievements become possible. This mirrors personal and professional growth where basics form the platform for advanced success.

At-Home Challenge of the Week

Precision Side Kick: Place a small, soft target—such as a sticky note or plush toy—on a wall at a safe height. Students stand at a marked point and attempt to tap the target gently with the Side Kick. This builds accuracy, balance, and control while improving technique.

Parent-Child Bonding Idea

Be My Sidekick: Join your child in a playful superhero-themed activity. Dress as superheroes (or invent your own identities) and practice Side Kicks together. Let your child “train” you like a sidekick, boosting their confidence and making practice fun. This blends imagination, physical activity, and learning.
