Lesson Plan
Full-Turn Round Kicks

Updated: 11/30/2025

Objectives

This week will focus on Round Kicks. Students will learn the Full-Turn Roundhouse Kick technique. Most other moves and skills are still done and practiced, though the emphasis is on the lesson objectives.

Full-Turn Round Kicks: The proper Round Kick technique will be taught. Kicks are initially taught to be done with the back leg, as this is generally more difficult. Students will learn the small details regarding exactly how their bodies should move. We will then practice a full turn (which is a 180-degree shift in their stance). They will be taught to kick and land their foot all the way back to where it started. Round Kicks are actually a very difficult kick to do completely correctly.

Round kicks in this style are separated into two distinct variations: a Full-turn round kick, and an Upward round kick. This lesson is focused on the former whereas another lesson covers the latter. A Full-turn round kick is the classic roundhouse kick (used often in Taekwondo), whereas the Upward round kick is seen more in Thai kickboxing. A proper Round Kick requires good balance, agility, and muscular control. In class we will use the clapper pad, shield, and thunder paper to practice our round kicks.

A full turn round kick delivers an entirely sideways attack. This is a lateral strike that is done at a 90-degree angle (assuming 0-degree is straight up from the floor). This can be done with the top of the foot or the shin, or both. Generally the shin is reserved for upward round kicks. This kick is best done in either a guarding stance or a front stance with the back leg, and a side stance with the lead leg.

However, a lead leg kick can be done easily in a guarding stance if the practitioner transitions to a side stance for the move, which is a simple pivot and twist of the hips. Back leg round kicks can generate a lot of power. Standing in a guarding stance will increase power when twisting the hips, but is the slowest way to do this kick. The preparation (chamber) is a bent leg with the toes pointed. The supporting foot will face the back, as found in a side stance.

For this kick to work, your body and hips must be completely sideways as the strike lands. If you kick with the back leg, you have to pivot your front foot and turn your hips. It’s better to pivot by first lifting up the heel and then turning on the ball of the foot.

Drills

Kicking In a Line: Students will line up single file for full-turn round kicks. These will be done stationary (using the same leg). With every full-turn round kick, if it is done with the back leg, the turn of the body moves us forward. So it becomes easy to practice by alternating legs and moving down the floor with our kicks. Our primary focus for this drill, though, will be on back leg kicks landing in the back. After each student has completed a few kicks, they will quickly run to the back of the line and get into a guarding stance.

Kicking Down The Floor: Students line up horizontally and get into a guarding stance. Using the space provided they will kick down the floor. We will start this drill with snap front kicks and move on to round kicks after. Each student will kick with their back leg and then land that kicking foot in the front. This way, each step is a kick and they will travel down the floor. This is an excellent way to naturally progress with footing, weight shifting, and the motions of kicking.

Games

Best Kicker: The instructor will hold a pad out for each kid to Full-Turn Round Kick. The class may be in a side-by-side line, or a semi-circle. Each student will do one kick and then freeze in their stance. After everyone has kicked, the instructor will announce the winner. To win, the student must execute the best kick and remain frozen in their stance. After a few rounds, the game may include other moves.

Nobody Wins: Students line up side by side in a guarding stance. When the instructor says “Kick!” each student must do a back leg full-turn round kick and land in the front in a new guarding stance. This way they will travel down the floor, alternating lead legs after each kick. Any mistake forces a restart. This game teaches the ideal of unattainable perfection and dramatically improves round kicks.

Words of Wisdom

People will often say that the best choice in defense is not to fight at all. In karate, evasions protect us and avoid all contact with our opponents. In life, this should not be overlooked as a potential move when dealing with conflict. You are not obligated to meet or react to every stress, conflict, or argument. Sometimes, strategic avoidance is the best move, saving time, effort, and unnecessary stress.

At-Home Challenge of the Week

Swinging Target: Hang a ball from the ceiling with string or twine. Students practice striking the swinging target with full-turn round kicks, emphasizing proper pivoting and balance. Keep the height safe and comfortable to avoid slipping.

Parent-Child Bonding Idea

Teach A Technique: Have your child teach you everything they know about the full-turn round kick. Ask questions that prompt deeper thought. Teaching strengthens understanding and builds patience for both student and parent.
