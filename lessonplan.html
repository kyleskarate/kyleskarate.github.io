Lesson Plan
Hooks

Updated: 12/14/2025

Objectives

This week’s lesson is focused on Hooks. The class will explore the unique motion of a hooking punch and how to make proper contact. Students will also combine hooks with jabs and crosses to create combinations and free-flow chains. Our objectives are to understand the hook correctly and integrate it naturally into combinations.

Hook: A hook is a lateral, swinging punch. It has a short arc, making it fast and harder to predict. The fist aims sideways with the pinky side down. Twisting the shoulders and hips increases power. While power is beneficial, students are taught not to over-arc or swing too wide. The hook targets the sides of an opponent rather than straight forward like a jab. A wing block protects the head from this move.

Mid Stance: A Mid Stance is halfway between a Front Stance and Back (Guarding) Stance. It is neutral, allowing offense, defense, and quick maneuvering. Weight is evenly distributed. The lead foot faces forward, and the back foot angles slightly outward, making transitions smooth depending on the next technique.

Drills

Hooks: The instructor begins by demonstrating the correct hook motion. Students learn the difference between a lateral haymaker and a proper hook, understanding why the arm must bend. The class practices slowly as a group with feedback. Afterward, each student performs hooks on focus mitts with both arms, beginning lightly to ensure proper form.

Combinations: After practicing hooks individually, techniques are combined into short chains. Up to now, students have performed Jab–Cross. Combinations help them think of a sequence as one unit, improving flow. The instructor assigns randomized sequences such as:

  • Jab–Jab–Hook
  • Jab–Cross–Hook–Hook

Students practice alternating hands, adjusting rhythm, speed, and power.

Free Flow Combinations (Hand Strikes Only): Students take turns striking as the instructor holds mitts at varying heights and distances. They must choose their own techniques, experimenting with rhythm, power, and timing. They insert light jabs or fakes before stronger hooks and crosses. The instructor occasionally swipes at their head, prompting evasions such as ducking, bobbing, weaving, fading, and tilting.

Games

Last One in Gets the Win: Students line up and take turns performing a chosen combination on the mitts, immediately followed by an evasion. Failure to evade results in elimination. As the number of students decreases, the pace intensifies. The last remaining student wins.

Words of Wisdom

Today's focus on hooks is a great metaphor for life’s unexpected challenges. A hook’s curved trajectory reminds us that not everything comes straight at us. Many problems require an angled approach. Like a hook, effective solutions often come from technique and strategy rather than brute strength. Be adaptable and ready to change direction when needed. Sometimes the greatest impact comes from the angle you didn’t expect.

At-Home Challenge of the Week

100 Hooks: Students practice 100 hooks in a single day, alternating sides while counting. Emphasis is placed on deliberate form—quality over speed.

Parent-Child Bonding Idea

Hooks and Hands: Parents hold out open palms for basic punches: jabs, crosses, straights. When the palm is sideways, the student should throw a hook. Once they improve, the parent occasionally swings an open palm toward their head so the child can practice covering or evading. Parents may sit while the child stands, or move around the room—ensuring safety to avoid accidental facial punches.
